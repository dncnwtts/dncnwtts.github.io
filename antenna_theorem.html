The Antenna Theorem

This is a derivation of the semi-famous antenna theorem, which can be summarized by the two equations \[ \int_{4\pi}A_e\,d\Omega=\lambda^2\,\qquad \langle A_e\rangle=\frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi}. \] Here I follow the NRAO radio astronomy courses's notes, which are a great resource for someone like me who knows a fair amount of cosmology but little of how measurements are made.

The first concept is that of the effective collecting area $A_e$ of an antenna. The total spectral energy, integrated over frequency, that a receiver collects will be the product of the geometric area $A$ and the flux density $S$. A real antenna that collects spectral energy $P_\nu$ has an effective area, \[ A_e\equiv\frac{P_\nu}{S_\text{matched}} \] where $S_\text{matched}$ is the flux density of a matched polarization, i.e. only the energy from one direction of polarization. For an unpolarized source, this is just half of the flux density. Therefore, \[ P_\nu=A_eS_\text{matched}=A_e\frac S2=\int_{4\pi}A_e(\theta,\phi)\frac{B_\nu}{2}d\Omega \] where $S_\nu=B_\nu$ since we're taking the antenna to be in thermal equilibrium. I belive that in this last equation, we had implicitly defined $A_e=\int_{4\pi}A_e(\theta,\phi)d\Omega$. It is known that a warm resister creates spectral power \[ P_\nu=\frac{h\nu}{\exp(h\nu/kT)-1}=kT\frac{h\nu/kT}{\exp(h\nu/kT)-1}\simeq kT \] for low frequencies (i.e. radio frequencies). The blackbody term, conversely, is given by \[ B_\nu=\frac{2kT\nu^2}{c^2}\frac{h\nu/kT}{\exp(h\nu/kT)-1}\simeq\frac{2kT\nu^2}{c^2} \] Using this, we can find the integrated area, \[ \int_{4\pi}A_e\,d\Omega=\frac{2P_\nu}{B_\nu}=kT\frac{c^2}{kT\nu^2}=\lambda^2 \] which means that the average collecting area is given by \[ \langle A_e\rangle=\frac{\int_{4\pi}A_e\,d\Omega}{\int_{4\pi}d\Omega}=\frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi} \] Note that this works only for lossless antennas, but it also shows that you want to use antennas to work with long wavelengths to get good reception. Note that any antenna, no matter the size, has the same average collecting area, at that wavelength.

Also note that time reversal basically lets us run things in reverse, i.e. you can look at a receiver or an antenna and get the same equations; this lets us relate the gain $G(\theta,\phi)$ (the proportion of energy being thrown out of the antenna) to $A_e$; \[ \langle A_e\rangle=\frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi},\qquad\langle G\rangle=1\Rightarrow A_e(\theta,\phi)=\frac{\lambda^2G(\theta,\phi)}{4\pi} \]
