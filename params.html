How $\boldsymbol{H_0}$ is determined

The main way the CMB determines cosmological parameters is by measuring $\theta_*$, which is the angular scale of the BAO at the redshift of decoupling $z_*$. Briefly, \[ \theta_*=\frac{r_s(z_*)}{D_A(z_*)}. \] The way that this is determined essentially comes down to the ratio of the baryon and cold dark matter density $\omega_b\equiv \Omega_bh^2$ and $\omega_c\equiv \Omega_c h^2$, which are both highly degenerate with $h=H_0/(100\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}\,Mpc^{-1}})$. Crucially, the expansion history comes into this, and can be determined however loosely through this. The details of this are complicated, but we essentially get a determination of the expansion rate at the time of decoupling $H_*$. Recall that \[ H(z)^2/H_0^2=\Omega_m(1+z)^3+\Omega_r(1+z)^4+\Omega_\Lambda+\Omega_k(1+z)^2. \] Clearly $H_0$ and $z$ are inversely correlated, and $H_0$ and the density of any parameter are also inversely correlated. One potential avenue for a $\mathrm{\Lambda CDM}$ extension is adding new relativistic species, $N_\mathrm{eff}>3.046$. This allegedly would increase the $H_0$ from the CMB. On its face, this would increase $\Omega_r$ at high redshifts, lowering $H_0$. However, increasing $\Omega_r$ also decreases the redshift of matter-radiation equality \[ 1+z_\mathrm{eq}=\frac{\Omega_m}{\Omega_r}. \] This in turn will lower $z_*$. Clearly then, if from the CMB we determine some $H_*\equiv H(z_*)$, we would infer the quanity \[ H_0=\frac{H_*}{\Omega_m(1+z_*)^3+\Omega_r(1+z_*)^4+\Omega_\Lambda+\Omega_k(1+z_*)^2}. \] With a fixed $H_*$, determined essentially from $\theta_*$, lowering $z_*$ would increase $H_0$'s value.
